Design process

Designing a pouch across two time zones.

By Dawon Kim · Apr 2026 · 5 min read

The first pouch was wrong. So was the second.

I sketched the first shape in Seoul on a Tuesday. Alex saw it Wednesday morning in LA. We're sixteen hours apart. That gap ended up being the thing that made the pouch good.

You can't hand something to someone the same day across that gap. You have to let it sit. The file goes to sleep with one of us and wakes up with the other. It gets read by fresh eyes before anyone defends it. I learned to send the work and walk away. That alone killed maybe twenty bad ideas.

The pouch is 100 mL. That's the constraint everything else answers to. It has to fit in a pocket, survive a bag, stand up on a shelf, feel warm in your hand, not look clinical, not look childish, and say PHEEEW loud enough without shouting. We tried it taller. Too precious. We tried it wider. Too much. We landed somewhere between a sauce packet and a small juice box, and once we saw it, we stopped trying to make it something else.

You can't hand something to someone the same day across that gap. You have to let it sit.

The wordmark went through thirty rounds. I won't show them. Most were embarrassing. The one we kept is three E's on purpose. One E is a word. Two E's are a joke. Three E's are a sound. We wanted the sound — the little release you make after a long sip of something cold when it actually worked. Phew. But longer. Pheeew.

The color came last. We tried a lot of brown. We tried a lot of cream. The thing that tipped it was a photo Alex sent from a farmers market in Atwater — an old orange awning over a stack of peaches. The light coming through was exactly the tone we'd been chasing in Figma for six weeks. I built the palette from that photo the next morning in Seoul. It's warmer than I would have made it on my own. It's better because of that.

Design for a consumer product is not about making something beautiful. It's about making something that doesn't need to be explained. If you have to tell someone what it is, you've already lost. The pouch has to do the work. The wordmark has to do the work. The color has to do the work. You just get out of its way.

I'm still in Seoul. Alex is still in LA. We still send the work across the gap. It's still the thing that makes it good.

— Dawon
